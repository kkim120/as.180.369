What is the Fisher Effect?

$$ (1 + i) = (1 + r) (1 + _{e}) $$

$$ i r + _{e} $$

Distinction

  1. Expected Inflation Component

    • If expected inflation () increases, the nominal interest rate (i) will increase, assuming the real interest rate remains constant
    • Investment strategies: seek higher returns to offset the anticipated erosion of purchasing power
    • Stock prices: negative response, as investors demand a higher real return on their investment

  2. Unexpected Inflation Component

    • Impacts the real interest rate (r). If nominal interest rates remain unchanged, the real interest rate effectively changes
    • Uncertainty: lead to market volatility
    • Stock prices: negative response, as investors react to the unforeseen changes in the economic landscape

    • Stock return: Schwert's 1990 data source, which mirrors a market index of U.S and U.K
    • Long Horizon: arbitrary 5 years

Summary of the literature

  1. Fama and Schwert
    • One month Expected Inflation (T-Bills) linear regression on short-term stock returns
    • Very Negative
  2. Domian, Gilster, and Louton
    • One month Expected Inflation (T-bills) times series regression on short-term stock returns
    • Still negative
  3. Boudoukh and Richardson
    • Inflation data (BLS data of 1 year inflation to 5 years) on long-term (1 to 5 years) stock returns
    • positive; contradicts previous works
